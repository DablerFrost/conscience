The Pledge

Net Zero by 2050. A 30% Intensity Reduction by 2035. No Scope 3 Target.

Shell's stated climate commitment is net zero emissions by 2050, covering Scope 1, 2, and 3. The near-term mechanism is a 30% reduction in emissions intensity by 2035 compared to 2016 levels, measured as net carbon intensity — grams of CO₂-equivalent per megajoule of energy sold. Shell has not committed to a science-based target through the SBTi. It has not filed a 1.5°C-aligned target. The 2035 intensity commitment is its primary near-term climate metric.

Shell reported that by 2023, it had reduced its Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 31% compared to 2016 — nominally on track for the 2035 target. That figure does not capture what matters most about an oil and gas company's climate impact: the emissions from the fuel it sells and the customers who burn it. Scope 3 emissions represent more than 90% of Shell's total lifecycle footprint.

"The court acknowledges that RDS cannot solve this global problem on its own. However, this does not absolve RDS of its individual partial responsibility to do its part regarding the emissions of the Shell group, which it can control and influence."

— Hague District Court, May 26, 2021 · Milieudefensie v Shell
The Intensity Metric

Reducing Intensity Is Not the Same as Reducing Emissions.

Shell's primary near-term climate target is measured in emissions intensity per unit of energy sold — not in absolute tonnes of CO₂. This distinction is not semantic. It is structural. An intensity metric can improve while absolute emissions grow, if the company sells more total energy. This is the core accountability gap in Shell's climate strategy.

Intensity vs Absolute — Why It Matters
Intensity target measures CO₂ per unit of energy sold
Absolute target measures Total CO₂ emitted — all scopes
If volume increases 20% and intensity drops 10% Absolute emissions still rise 8%
Paris Agreement requires Absolute reductions — all sectors
Shell's SBTi commitment Not committed

The 2021 Hague District Court understood this distinction. It ordered Shell to achieve a 45% absolute reduction in net CO₂ emissions by 2030 — including Scope 3 — relative to 2019 levels. The court's order was precisely calibrated to the gap between intensity metrics and what climate science actually requires. Shell appealed.

Milieudefensie v Shell · 2019–2026

The Landmark Case That Changed Climate Litigation — And Its Partial Reversal.

The Milieudefensie case is the most consequential corporate climate lawsuit ever brought. Filed in April 2019 by Friends of the Earth Netherlands and 17,000 co-plaintiffs, it asked the Hague District Court to order Shell to align its climate strategy with the Paris Agreement. In May 2021, the court ruled in favour of the plaintiffs in a world first — a private company was legally ordered to reduce its emissions under a tort-law duty of care. Shell appealed.

Case Timeline · Milieudefensie et al v Royal Dutch Shell
Apr 2019 Milieudefensie files suit in the Hague District Court. Claim: Shell's failure to reduce emissions violates a duty of care to Dutch citizens under Article 6:162 of the Dutch Civil Code. Filed
May 2021 Hague District Court orders Shell to reduce net CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 vs 2019 levels — including Scope 3. World's first order requiring a private company to align with Paris Agreement. Shell appeals. Landmark
Nov 2024 Hague Court of Appeal overturns the 45% reduction mandate. Ruling: a specific percentage derived from global climate frameworks cannot be imposed on a single company without individual context. However — the court affirms Shell has a duty of care to reduce all-scope emissions. Shell's investments in new fossil fuel production "may violate" the duty of care. Overturned
Feb 2025 Milieudefensie announces appeal to the Dutch Supreme Court. Litigation ongoing. The duty-of-care principle established in 2021 — that private companies have legal obligations to mitigate climate change — was not overturned. Pending

The November 2024 ruling is not a clean win for Shell. The appeals court affirmed the core legal principle: companies have a duty of care under Dutch law to mitigate dangerous climate change across all emission scopes. It declined to impose a specific percentage, citing the difficulty of applying a global Paris target to a single company without tailored analysis. Milieudefensie lost on this specific claim — not on the underlying legal theory. The Supreme Court appeal means the case is not resolved.

Of particular note: the appeals court observed that Shell's investments in new fossil fuel production activities "could be at odds with its duty of care" to address dangerous climate change. This observation was made while declining to make it the basis of an order — because the specific claim before the court was about the 45% reduction standard, not about investment strategy. Future litigation may be structured around precisely that question.

CONSCIENCE Verdict

19/100. An Intensity Target Is Not a Climate Commitment.

Shell scores 19/100. The score reflects a company that discloses more than most oil majors, that has made genuine operational improvements on Scope 1 and 2 emissions, and that is simultaneously using a metric designed to allow continued volume growth while claiming climate progress. The intensity target is the defining accountability gap.

The Milieudefensie case is the most important piece of context in Shell's CONSCIENCE record. A court ordered 45% absolute cuts. Shell appealed. The appeals court overturned the specific mandate while affirming the underlying legal duty. The Supreme Court case is pending. Whatever the legal outcome, the case has established one precedent that will not be reversed: a private oil company's climate obligations are justiciable under national tort law. That principle is now embedded in European climate litigation.

Shell's continued investment in new fossil fuel upstream production — acknowledged by the appeals court as potentially problematic — is the structural tension at the centre of its climate narrative. Companies can reduce intensity while growing volume. The atmosphere responds to absolute tonnes, not ratios.
